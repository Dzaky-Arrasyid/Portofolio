Those small diagnostic wins are what drew me to this discipline in the first place, and they're what I'm looking forward to doing more of, at a larger scale, in a full-time role.

I recently completed my Applied Bachelor (D-IV) in Refinery Instrumentation Engineering at Politeknik Energi dan Mineral Akamigas (PEM Akamigas) in Cepu, specializing in instrumentation, process control, and automation.

What I enjoy most is the hands-on side of the work. Across internships in geothermal power and gold processing, I've calibrated pressure, temperature, level and flow transmitters, tuned PID control loops with live step tests, performed loop checks on 4–20 mA signals, and troubleshot control valves, RTDs and three-phase motors out on the plant floor. I care about getting measurements right, because everything downstream — safety, efficiency, decisions — depends on them.

I'm comfortable moving between the control room and the field: reading P&IDs, working on DCS and SCADA platforms like Yokogawa CENTUM VP, and building IoT monitoring systems from sensor to dashboard. Now I'm looking for a role where I can keep learning and contribute to reliable, well-instrumented operations.

A bit more on how I got here: my interest in instrumentation actually started outside the classroom, tinkering with Arduino and ESP32-based IoT projects — things like remote fuel-level monitoring and water-level control systems. That hobby turned into a habit of breaking things down into sensor, signal, logic, and output, which turned out to map almost directly onto how industrial instrumentation works. By the time I got to my internships, that mental model made it much easier to pick up real plant instruments quickly.
